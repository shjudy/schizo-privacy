English SCHIZO is a Mac app that turns a song recorded alone in a room into a finished record — stems, pitch, tone, harmonies, mix and master. All audio processing stays on the user's Mac. 1. What the app does • Splits a track into vocals and instrumental (on-device AI stem separation, 4 stems). • Diagnoses a take against the original and lets you fix notes on a piano roll. • Reshapes the texture, tone and breath of a voice in real time, and clears noise, mouth sounds, room reverb and clipping. • Reads the key of a song and stacks harmonies (key changes detected automatically). • Builds a mix with doubles, choir, space and density (real-time preview plus automation curves). • Masters toward the tone and loudness of commercial records, and lets you hear a commercial color and your own side by side before choosing. • Every step is kept on a non-destructive timeline (the "chart"), so any version can be recalled with its exact settings and worked on further. • Simple Mode for first-timers — one file, one knob, one button. 2. Why this world SCHIZO's world did not borrow illness as a theme; it came out of what recording alone actually requires. Turning one song into a record means one person taking turns at seven different judgments — separation, pitch, tone, harmony, mix, master, verdict — and the app gives those seven hands seven faces. The darkness here is a metaphor, not a joke, and the evidence sits inside the app: a column of 92 short factual cards on schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, depression, anxiety and panic disorder, social anxiety, PTSD and insomnia, all drawn from public materials from public-health authorities (WHO, Korea's national mental-health information portal, NIMH), each labeled by condition and carrying a visible disclaimer ("based on public sources · may vary from person to person · not medical advice"). A single Calm Mode switch in the header turns every narrative effect off, the onboarding can be skipped, and that onboarding carries a persistent notice that this is a music-production tool and the story is fiction. The story is built to be peeled back one layer per update — what you can see today is not all of it. 3. The developer SCHIZO is made by one person — design, DSP, UI, art selection and copy. Shyun.D, which appears outside the app (share-card watermarks, exports, release notes, the foot of documents), is the signature of the person who made it, always at the end, in the form Made with SCHIZO — Shyun.D. 4. Privacy in short • Audio is local only. Audio files, recordings and results are never sent to a server under any circumstances, and every processing step (stem separation, pitch correction, noise and reverb removal, mixing, mastering) runs on the user's Mac. File names and song titles are not transmitted either. • Sign-in is optional. Every feature works without an account; signing in is only used to back up settings and preferences. • Usage statistics are opt-in. Choosing "Don't send" on the first-launch consent screen means nothing is ever transmitted, and it can be turned off later at any time — doing so also deletes anything still queued on the device. The values sent are only non-identifying numbers, booleans and in-app screen names. • No ad identifiers, no tracking, and no selling or sharing of data with third parties for advertising. • No subscription. There is a single non-consumable in-app purchase, and Apple handles the payment itself (the developer receives no amounts, transaction IDs or account details). • Account info and settings backups can be erased instantly with the in-app "Delete account" button. • Full text: Privacy Policy 5. Frequently asked Q. Why is the app called SCHIZO? In English that word is a slur. I chose it knowingly. That is the most important thing in this answer. I do not have schizophrenia. What I do have is an illness that is classified as a mental illness, and more than ten years of counseling and treatment for it. Over those years one thing kept striking me as strange: people can say out loud that they have other illnesses, but with this family of illnesses almost everyone is busy hiding it. There were stretches where not being able to say it sat heavier than the illness did. So I had a thought. What if this word could become ordinary — not a compliment, not an insult, just "ah, that is someone who has that illness," the way "cancer patient" lands. I thought that putting the word on a title might push a little in that direction. I am aware of what that sounds like. This word is used against people, and I am not one of the people it is used against; I do not think reclaiming someone else's label is something a bystander gets to do on their behalf. So I did not rest the case on the name. I put the evidence inside the app instead — 92 information cards about mental illness, each with its source shown; a Calm Mode that switches the entire fiction off for anyone who does not want it; and an onboarding that keeps a standing notice saying the story is fiction. The characters take their names from musical markings, not from diagnoses. I do not know yet whether this was the right call. If I hear that it hurts, I will think about it again. Q. Why build the app around mental illness at all? Please read "2. Why this world" above as the answer. To add one thing: the rules for not turning symptoms into a spectacle are written down inside the codebase — no depiction of self-harm or suicide, no jokes about weight or appearance, softened phrasing, an explicit condition label, a stated source, and a visual separation between narrative lines and medical-information lines. Every new card has to pass the same rules. Q. Is this a medical app? No. SCHIZO is a music-production tool. It does not diagnose, does not treat, and does not give medical advice. The information cards inside the app are educational summaries based on public materials from public-health authorities, and every card carries the disclaimer "may vary from person to person · not medical advice". Q. Do you use AI? Yes, and where it is used is fully disclosed in the notices document reachable from the app's account screen. (1) Audio — stem separation runs the open-source htdemucs model (Demucs v4, Meta AI, MIT) converted to Core ML, on the device; room-reverb removal downloads a neural model and also runs it on the device. User audio is never used for training and never leaves the Mac. (2) Art — character art, onboarding and background images and puzzle pieces were produced with generative image tools, then selected, cropped and retouched by hand. No existing third-party work was taken. The signature lettering was made the same way. The three UI sounds are synthesized tones with no external samples, and the genre target data, information-card copy and presets are all made in-house. (3) Voice — the app does not generate voices. There is no feature that imitates or transplants someone else's voice; the only thing processed is what the user actually sang (even the "side voice" is derived from the user's own take). Q. Where does my data go? Audio goes nowhere — the entire processing chain finishes on the user's Mac, and file names and song titles are not transmitted. The only things that reach a server are (1) the account identifier, email and settings backup if you sign in, and (2) anonymous usage statistics if you consent. Both are optional and can be turned off or deleted at any time. The server is Supabase and the data is stored in the Seoul region. Details are in "4. Privacy in short" above and in the privacy policy. Q. Why a one-time purchase instead of a subscription? Selling a tool felt more honest than renting it out. Every feature is free to use; only saving finished tracks is limited to one song. There is a single non-consumable in-app purchase that lifts that limit, and no subscription. Q. Can it be used to separate someone else's commercial recording? This is a tool built for files you have the rights to, and its purpose is not to strip someone else's record but to turn your own voice into one. Whether a given use is lawful is up to the user.
